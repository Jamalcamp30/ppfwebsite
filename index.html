How we build the profile

The room is built around what scouts, families, and serious athletes actually care about.

We keep the standard precise and uncompromising. We coach acceleration, top-end speed, jump mechanics, force application, positional movement, recovery, and testing rhythm with the expectation that every athlete presents cleanly on paper and even better in person.

Speed that carries

Start angles, shin position, front-side mechanics, projection, stiffness, and clean ground contact are coached so the stopwatch reflects the work. The result is speed that holds its shape when the testing gets real.

Power that transfers

We tie weight-room output to jumps, force production, violent first steps, and movement that shows up in both testing and position drills. Strength is expected to carry into the field, not stay trapped in the rack.

Position detail

Wideouts, defensive backs, linemen, linebackers, backs, and specialists do not need the same conversation. That matters here. Position detail stays specific so the work carries over cleanly to the athlete on the board.

Readiness between sessions

Nutrition, recovery, and sports-medicine support stay connected to the work because real development does not stop when the set ends. Athletes are expected to recover with the same discipline they bring to training.

A combine-caliber benchmark that would have moved the board.

At Georgia Tech Pro Day in 2021, Jalen Camp posted 30 reps on the 225-pound bench press. The NFL Combine wide receiver record at the time was 27 reps, set in 2019. If Camp had been in Indianapolis that year, his number would have cleared the previous mark by 3 reps.

It is the kind of carryover that matters because size, power, and verified output all show up in one result.
